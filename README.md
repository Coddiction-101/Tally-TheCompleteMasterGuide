## 1. What Is Tally — Really?

Tally is an accounting and business management software developed by Tally Solutions Pvt. Ltd., headquartered in Bangalore, India. It is the most widely used accounting software in India and is dominant across South Asia, the Middle East, and parts of Africa.

But calling Tally just "accounting software" is like calling Microsoft Excel just a "spreadsheet." Let's go deeper.

Tally is a complete business management system. It handles financial accounting, inventory management, statutory compliance (GST, TDS, TCS), payroll, banking, budgeting, and reporting — all in one integrated platform. A small shopkeeper in a village and a mid-sized manufacturing company with 500 employees can both use Tally to run their entire financial operations.

What makes Tally unique compared to global alternatives like QuickBooks or SAP is that it was built from the ground up for the Indian business environment — deeply integrated with Indian tax laws, compliance requirements like GST filing, TDS returns, and the way Indian businesses actually operate. This is why it has remained dominant in India for over three decades despite intense competition.

Tally works on a concept called double-entry bookkeeping — every financial transaction affects at least two accounts, ensuring the books always balance. You do not need to know accounting theory to use Tally, but understanding it deeply helps you use Tally far more effectively. That is why Section 4 of this guide covers accounting fundamentals before touching the software.

At its core, Tally does one thing exceptionally well: it takes the complex world of business finance and makes it manageable, auditable, and compliant. Every rupee that enters or leaves a business can be tracked, categorized, reported, and submitted to the government — all through Tally.

## 2. The History and Evolution of Tally

Understanding the history of Tally helps you appreciate why it is designed the way it is and why it matters so much in the Indian business context.

**1986 — The Beginning.** Bharat Goenka, the founder of Tally, wrote the first version of the software for his father's business. It was not even called Tally at first — it was simply an accounting tool written in Basic programming language to solve a real business problem. The focus was always practical utility, not theoretical elegance.

**1988 — Tally 3.0.** The product was commercially released as Peutronics Financial Accountant. It ran on MS-DOS and could handle basic bookkeeping.

**1990 — Tally 4.5.** The company was renamed Tally Solutions and the product matured significantly, adding features for Indian businesses including multi-currency support.

**1996 — Tally 5.4.** Windows support arrived, making Tally accessible to a far wider audience as computers became more common in Indian businesses.

**1999 — Tally 6.3.** Introduced a revolutionary concept at the time — concurrent multi-user access, meaning multiple people in a business could use the software simultaneously over a network.

**2001 — Tally 7.2.** Added VAT (Value Added Tax) compliance — the first major statutory compliance integration. This was a turning point because it showed that Tally could handle government tax requirements natively, which became its defining competitive advantage.

**2005 — Tally 8.0 and 9.0.** Introduced payroll processing and more sophisticated inventory management.

**2009 — Tally ERP 9.** The most significant version in Tally's history. ERP stands for Enterprise Resource Planning. Tally ERP 9 added remote access, multi-company management, advanced inventory, comprehensive statutory compliance, and a dramatically improved user interface. It became the dominant version and was used by millions of businesses for over a decade.

**2017 — GST Integration.** When India introduced GST (Goods and Services Tax) on July 1, 2017, Tally was ready on day one. Overnight, millions of businesses needed GST-compliant accounting software, and Tally was already prepared. This cemented Tally's market leadership.

**2020 — TallyPrime.** The newest generation of Tally. TallyPrime is not just an update to ERP 9 — it is a complete redesign with a new user interface, better navigation, connected services (online filing, bank statement import), and a significantly improved user experience. TallyPrime is the present and future of Tally.

Today, Tally is used by over 7.5 million businesses globally, with the vast majority in India.

## 3. Tally ERP 9 vs TallyPrime — Key Differences

Since your curriculum may reference either version, here is a clear comparison.

**The Navigation Model.** Tally ERP 9 uses a keyboard-first, menu-driven interface that feels dated by modern standards but is extremely fast once memorized. TallyPrime introduces a completely redesigned interface with a top menu bar, context-sensitive help, and far more intuitive navigation. The underlying accounting logic is identical — only the user experience differs.

**Go To Feature.** TallyPrime's most celebrated feature. Instead of navigating through multiple menu levels to find a report or function, you can press Ctrl+G or simply start typing what you want ("Day Book", "Balance Sheet", "GST Report") and TallyPrime takes you there instantly. ERP 9 has no equivalent.

**Report Customization.** TallyPrime allows you to customize any report — filter it, change columns, add or remove data — directly from the report screen without going into separate configuration menus. ERP 9 requires more steps.

**Connected Services.** TallyPrime introduces Tally.NET services — online filing directly from Tally, bank statement import and reconciliation, remote access, and SMS/email reports directly from the software. ERP 9 had limited connectivity.

**Multi-Tasking.** TallyPrime allows you to open multiple reports and entry screens simultaneously and switch between them. ERP 9 is single-task — you must close what you are doing before opening something else.

**Licensing.** TallyPrime changed from ERP 9's perpetual license model (buy once, use forever) to a subscription model for new features, though existing licenses were grandfathered.

**For Your Learning.** The accounting concepts, ledger structure, voucher types, GST handling, and reports work identically in both versions. If you learn one, switching to the other requires only learning the new navigation — the knowledge transfers completely. For this guide, concepts are described in a version-agnostic way with version-specific notes where the interface differs significantly.

## 4. Accounting Fundamentals You Must Know First

This is the most important section for a complete beginner. Tally is a tool that implements accounting. If you do not understand the accounting concepts underneath, you will click buttons without understanding what they mean, make errors you cannot debug, and never truly master the software.

Read this section carefully. It will make everything in Tally click into place.

### The Purpose of Accounting

Accounting is the systematic process of recording, classifying, summarizing, and interpreting all financial transactions of a business. Its purpose is to answer three fundamental questions at any point in time: How much does the business own? How much does the business owe? How much profit or loss has it made?

### The Accounting Equation

The entire foundation of double-entry bookkeeping rests on one equation:

**Assets = Liabilities + Capital (Owner's Equity)**

This equation must always balance. Always. Every single transaction you record in Tally maintains this balance automatically. Understanding why it always balances is the key to understanding accounting.

Assets are what the business owns — cash, bank balance, inventory, machinery, buildings, money owed to the business by customers (debtors/receivables).

Liabilities are what the business owes — loans, money owed to suppliers (creditors/payables), outstanding expenses.

Capital is what the owner has invested in the business plus accumulated profits minus withdrawals.

### Double-Entry Bookkeeping

Every financial transaction has two sides — it gives value to one account and takes value from another. This is called double-entry bookkeeping. The account that receives value is Debited. The account that gives value is Credited.

The fundamental rule: For every debit, there must be an equal and opposite credit. This is why the books always balance.

### The Golden Rules of Accounting

These three rules tell you when to debit and when to credit any account:

**Rule 1 — Personal Accounts** (accounts of people, companies, banks):
Debit the Receiver. Credit the Giver.
Example: You pay a supplier ₹10,000 cash. The supplier (personal account) receives money — Debit the supplier. Cash (personal account, yours) gives money — Credit cash.

**Rule 2 — Real Accounts** (accounts of things — cash, property, goods, assets):
Debit what comes in. Credit what goes out.
Example: You purchase machinery for ₹50,000 cash. Machinery comes in — Debit machinery. Cash goes out — Credit cash.

**Rule 3 — Nominal Accounts** (accounts of expenses, losses, income, gains):
Debit all expenses and losses. Credit all income and gains.
Example: You pay rent of ₹5,000. Rent is an expense — Debit rent. Cash goes out — Credit cash.

### Key Financial Terms You Will Use Daily in Tally

**Debit (Dr):** The left side of an account. Increases assets and expenses. Decreases liabilities, capital, and income.

**Credit (Cr):** The right side of an account. Increases liabilities, capital, and income. Decreases assets and expenses.

**Ledger:** A record of all transactions related to a particular account. Every unique entity in your accounting — a bank account, a customer, a supplier, an expense type — has its own ledger.

**Journal Entry / Voucher:** The record of a financial transaction showing which accounts are debited and credited and by how much.

**Trial Balance:** A list of all ledger balances at a point in time. Total debits must equal total credits.

**Profit and Loss Account (P&L):** Shows all income and expenses over a period, resulting in net profit or net loss.

**Balance Sheet:** A snapshot of the business's financial position at a specific date — what it owns (assets) and what it owes (liabilities + capital).

**Debtor:** A person or company that owes money TO your business (they bought from you on credit).

**Creditor:** A person or company to whom your business owes money (you bought from them on credit).

**Journal:** The book of original entry where transactions are first recorded chronologically.

**Contra Entry:** A transaction involving two cash/bank accounts — for example, depositing cash into the bank. Both accounts are in-house accounts. In Tally, this uses the Contra voucher type.

**Opening Balance:** The balance in an account at the start of a financial year.

**Closing Balance:** The balance in an account at the end of a financial year.

## 5. How Tally Works — The Core Architecture

Before touching the software, understand the conceptual structure of how Tally organizes financial data.

### The Company

In Tally, everything exists inside a "Company." A company is a separate entity with its own chart of accounts, transactions, and financial year. You can create multiple companies in one Tally installation — for example, if you manage accounts for three different businesses, each is a separate company in Tally.

A company has a financial year (in India, April 1 to March 31), a base currency, and configuration for statutory features like GST and TDS.

### The Chart of Accounts — Groups and Ledgers

Tally organizes all accounts in a hierarchical structure: Groups contain Ledgers.

A **Group** is a category of accounts. Tally comes with 28 pre-defined groups that mirror standard accounting classification — Capital Account, Bank Accounts, Sundry Debtors, Sundry Creditors, Sales Accounts, Purchase Accounts, and so on. You can create sub-groups.

A **Ledger** is an individual account. Every customer, every supplier, every bank account, every type of income or expense is a ledger. Ledgers always belong to a group. The group determines how the ledger appears in financial reports.

For example, the ledger "HDFC Bank Current Account" belongs to the group "Bank Accounts." The ledger "Office Rent" belongs to the group "Indirect Expenses." The ledger "Reliance Industries Ltd" (a customer) belongs to the group "Sundry Debtors."

This grouping is what makes Tally's automatic report generation possible. When Tally generates a Balance Sheet, it knows that Bank Accounts ledgers go in Current Assets because of the group they belong to.

### Vouchers

Every transaction in Tally is recorded through a Voucher. A voucher is the digital equivalent of a paper document recording a financial transaction. Different types of transactions use different voucher types — payments use Payment Vouchers, receipts use Receipt Vouchers, sales use Sales Vouchers, purchases use Purchase Vouchers, and so on. Tally has 18 voucher types built in, and you can create custom voucher types.

### The Flow of Data

When you record a voucher, Tally automatically updates every relevant ledger, creates journal entries, adjusts inventory (if applicable), calculates taxes, and reflects the change in every report — Balance Sheet, P&L, Trial Balance, GST returns — instantly and in real time. You never need to manually update reports. This is Tally's most powerful feature and one of the main reasons businesses use it.

## 7. Company Creation and Configuration

Creating a company in Tally is the foundational step. Every setting you configure here affects how the entire accounting system works.

### Creating a Company in TallyPrime

Press Alt+K (Company) → Create Company. You will see a form with the following fields:

**Company Name:** The legal name of the business. Example: "Sharma Traders" or "ABC Manufacturing Pvt Ltd." This appears on all printed documents.

**Primary Mailing Address:** The registered address of the business.

**Country:** India (for Indian businesses).

**State:** Select the state where the business is registered. This is important for GST — the state code is part of the GSTIN.

**Pin Code:** The postal code of the business address.

**Telephone and Email:** Contact details that appear on invoices and correspondence.

**Currency Symbol:** ₹ for Indian Rupee (default for Indian companies).

**Maintain:** Select "Accounts with Inventory" if the business deals in goods (trading, manufacturing, retail). Select "Accounts Only" for service businesses or pure financial accounting practice.

**Financial Year Beginning From:** In India, this is April 1. The financial year runs April 1 to March 31.

**Books Beginning From:** The date from which you will start entering transactions. For a new business, this is the same as the financial year beginning. For migrating from another system mid-year, this would be the migration date.

**Use Security Control:** Set to Yes if you want to add password protection to the company.

**Base Currency Information:** INR, symbol ₹, number of decimal places 2 (paise).

Press Ctrl+A or Enter to save. Tally creates the company and you are taken to the main accounting environment.

### Enabling Features and Statutory Settings

After creating the company, configure it further. In TallyPrime: Gateway → F11 (Features). In Tally ERP 9: Gateway → F11 (Company Features).

**Accounts Features (F1):** Enable Maintain Bill-wise Details if you want to track individual bills for debtors and creditors (essential for receivables management). Enable Cost Centres if you want to track expenses by department.

**Inventory Features (F2):** Enable if you have inventory. Set whether you want to use batch/lot numbers (for pharmaceuticals, food products), serial numbers (for electronics), or standard costing.

**Statutory and Taxation (F3):** This is where you enable GST, TDS, TCS, and Payroll. Enable Enable Goods and Services Tax (GST) and enter your GSTIN (15-character GST Identification Number). Enable Tax Deducted at Source (TDS) and Tax Collected at Source (TCS) if applicable.

### GST Configuration in Company

When you enable GST, fill in:
GSTIN/UIN: Your 15-digit GST number.
Applicable From: The date from which GST applies.
Periodicity of GSTR 1: Monthly or Quarterly depending on your turnover.

## 8. Ledgers — The Heart of Tally

Ledgers are the most fundamental element of Tally. Every entity involved in your accounting — every customer, every supplier, every bank account, every type of expense, every type of income — must be created as a ledger before you can record transactions involving it.

### Pre-Created Ledgers

When you create a new company, Tally automatically creates two ledgers: Cash (under Cash-in-Hand group) and Profit & Loss Account (under Primary group). These cannot be deleted.

### Creating a Ledger

In TallyPrime: Gateway → Accounts Info (or Create → Ledger).
In Tally ERP 9: Gateway of Tally → Accounts Info → Ledgers → Create.

**Name:** The name of the account. Make it clear and consistent. "HDFC Bank – Current A/c" is better than just "HDFC."

**Under (Group):** The most important field. The group you select determines where this ledger appears in financial reports. Selecting the wrong group is the most common beginner mistake. Use the table below as a reference.

| Ledger Type | Group to Select |
|-------------|----------------|
| Customer (who owes you money) | Sundry Debtors |
| Supplier (to whom you owe money) | Sundry Creditors |
| Bank account | Bank Accounts |
| Cash in hand | Cash-in-Hand |
| Sales income | Sales Accounts |
| Purchase expenses | Purchase Accounts |
| Rent, salary, utilities (expenses) | Indirect Expenses |
| Raw material purchases | Direct Expenses |
| Loans taken by business | Loans (Liability) |
| Loans given by business | Loans & Advances (Asset) |
| Owner's capital invested | Capital Account |
| Owner's withdrawal | Capital Account (or Drawings sub-group) |
| GST Output Tax | Duties & Taxes |
| GST Input Tax | Duties & Taxes |
| Machinery, furniture (fixed assets) | Fixed Assets |
| Stock/Inventory | Stock-in-Hand |

**Opening Balance:** If the ledger has an existing balance at the start of the financial year (for example, a customer owes ₹25,000 from last year), enter it here. For Dr/Cr — a customer (debtor) has a Debit opening balance. A supplier (creditor) has a Credit opening balance. A bank account has a Debit opening balance.

**Maintain Balances Bill-by-Bill:** Set to Yes for customer and supplier ledgers. This allows Tally to track which specific invoice is outstanding, enabling professional aging analysis and follow-up.

**Default Credit Period:** Enter the standard credit period you give this customer or have agreed with this supplier. For example, "30 Days."

**GST Details (TallyPrime):** For customer and supplier ledgers, specify whether they are Registered under GST (Regular), Unregistered, Consumer, etc. For registered parties, enter their GSTIN.

### Ledger Categories for Financial Reports

Understanding which group puts a ledger where in the Balance Sheet and P&L is critical:

**Balance Sheet — Assets Side:**
Fixed Assets → Fixed Assets, Plant & Machinery, Furniture, etc.
Current Assets → Sundry Debtors, Bank Accounts, Cash-in-Hand, Stock-in-Hand, Loans & Advances (Asset)

**Balance Sheet — Liabilities Side:**
Capital → Capital Account
Loans → Loans (Liability)
Current Liabilities → Sundry Creditors, Duties & Taxes

**Profit & Loss — Income Side:**
Sales Accounts, Other Income

**Profit & Loss — Expense Side:**
Purchase Accounts, Direct Expenses, Indirect Expenses

## 9. Groups in Tally

Tally has 28 pre-defined groups organized in a hierarchy. Understanding this hierarchy is essential because it determines how every account is classified in reports.

### Primary Groups (Top Level)

**Capital Account:** Owner's equity, capital invested, retained earnings.
**Loans (Liability):** Long-term and short-term borrowings.
**Current Liabilities:** Short-term obligations — creditors, outstanding expenses, advance payments received.
**Fixed Assets:** Long-term physical assets — land, buildings, machinery, furniture, vehicles.
**Investments:** Shares, bonds, mutual funds held by the business.
**Current Assets:** Short-term assets — debtors, cash, bank, inventory, prepaid expenses.
**Misc. Expenses (Asset):** Preliminary expenses, deferred revenue expenditure.
**Income / Revenue:** Under this, Sales Accounts and Other Income.
**Expenses:** Under this, Purchase Accounts, Direct Expenses, and Indirect Expenses.

### Understanding Direct vs Indirect Expenses

This distinction matters for the P&L format and for calculating Gross Profit vs Net Profit.

**Direct Expenses** are costs directly related to production or purchase of goods — raw materials, freight inward, direct labor, manufacturing wages. These appear in the Trading Account section of P&L and determine Gross Profit.

**Indirect Expenses** are costs related to running the business — rent, salaries of office staff, marketing, utilities, depreciation of office equipment, bank charges. These appear in the P&L section below Gross Profit and determine Net Profit.

Gross Profit = Sales – Cost of Goods Sold (Purchases + Direct Expenses ± Stock Changes)
Net Profit = Gross Profit – Indirect Expenses + Other Income

### Creating Custom Groups

If you need to sub-categorize accounts further — for example, creating a "Marketing Expenses" sub-group under Indirect Expenses — you can create custom groups. Go to Accounts Info → Groups → Create. Enter the group name and select the parent group it falls under.

## 10. Voucher Types and Transactions

Vouchers are how you record every financial transaction in Tally. Each voucher type serves a specific purpose. Using the wrong voucher type is a critical mistake because it misclassifies transactions.

### The 18 Built-In Voucher Types

**1. Contra (F4)**
Used for transactions between two cash or bank accounts within the business.
Examples: Depositing cash into the bank, withdrawing cash from the bank, transferring between bank accounts.
Both accounts must be cash or bank ledgers. This is what makes it a "contra" entry — it does not affect the P&L at all.
Entry: Cash deposited into HDFC Bank ₹10,000 → Dr HDFC Bank / Cr Cash

**2. Payment (F5)**
Used when the business makes a payment — paying cash or from bank account.
Examples: Paying rent, paying a supplier, paying salaries, paying utility bills.
Entry: Paying rent ₹8,000 by cheque → Dr Rent / Cr HDFC Bank

**3. Receipt (F6)**
Used when the business receives money — cash or into bank.
Examples: Receiving payment from a customer, receiving interest, receiving advance.
Entry: Customer Ramesh pays ₹15,000 → Dr Cash (or Bank) / Cr Ramesh

**4. Journal (F7)**
Used for non-cash, non-bank adjustments — entries that do not involve immediate money movement.
Examples: Recording depreciation, writing off bad debts, adjusting opening balances, accruals.
Entry: Depreciation on machinery ₹5,000 → Dr Depreciation / Cr Machinery

**5. Sales (F8)**
Used to record sales of goods or services. This is your invoice.
This voucher type creates a proper invoice that can be printed and sent to customers.
It updates inventory (reduces stock) and the customer's outstanding balance.
Entry: Sold goods to customer Priya for ₹25,000 + GST → Dr Priya / Cr Sales / Cr GST Output

**6. Credit Note (Ctrl+F8)**
Used to record sales returns — when a customer returns goods you previously sold.
It reverses a previous sales entry. This is the document you give the customer.
Entry: Priya returns goods worth ₹5,000 → Dr Sales Returns / Dr GST Output / Cr Priya

**7. Purchase (F9)**
Used to record purchases of goods from suppliers.
It updates inventory (increases stock) and the supplier's outstanding balance.
Entry: Purchased goods from Rajesh Traders for ₹18,000 + GST → Dr Purchases / Dr GST Input / Cr Rajesh Traders

**8. Debit Note (Ctrl+F9)**
Used to record purchase returns — when you return goods to a supplier.
Entry: Returned goods worth ₹3,000 to Rajesh Traders → Dr Rajesh Traders / Cr Purchase Returns / Cr GST Input

**9–18. Other Voucher Types:**
Memo (Non-Accounting): Provisional entry that doesn't affect accounts.
Order Vouchers: Sales Orders and Purchase Orders — record commitments to buy or sell before the actual transaction.
Delivery Note and Receipt Note: Record movement of goods without accounting entries.
Reversing Journal: Automatically reverses on a specified date.
Payroll Vouchers: For salary and attendance entries.

### Recording a Payment Voucher — Step by Step

This walkthrough applies broadly to all voucher types.

Open TallyPrime → Gateway → Vouchers → F5 (Payment).

**Date:** By default, Tally shows the last voucher date. Change it by pressing F2. Enter the transaction date.

**Account (in Single Entry Mode):** Select the bank or cash account from which payment is being made. Example: HDFC Bank.

**Particulars:** Select the ledger being debited (the expense or creditor being paid). Example: Office Rent.

**Amount:** Enter the amount. ₹8,000.

**Narration:** A brief description. Example: "Rent paid for April 2024 by cheque no. 001234."

Press Enter → Ctrl+A to save.

Tally instantly records: Debit Office Rent ₹8,000, Credit HDFC Bank ₹8,000. The Trial Balance, P&L, Balance Sheet, and Bank Book all update immediately.

### Recording a Sales Voucher with GST — Step by Step

Gateway → Vouchers → F8 (Sales).

**Party Name:** Select the customer ledger. If it doesn't exist, press Alt+C to create it on the spot.

**Sales Ledger:** Select the appropriate sales account (e.g., "Sales – Local 18%").

**Name of Item:** Select the stock item from your inventory.

**Quantity and Rate:** Enter quantity sold and rate per unit. Tally calculates amount.

**GST:** Tally automatically calculates GST based on the tax rate configured for the stock item. If selling locally (within same state) — CGST and SGST are calculated. If selling interstate — IGST is calculated.

**Total Amount:** Tally sums the taxable value and GST amounts.

Press Ctrl+A to save. Tally creates the invoice, updates inventory (stock decreases), updates the customer's ledger (Priya now owes you the full invoice amount), and records the GST liability.

## 11. Inventory Management in Tally

Tally's inventory management is a complete system for tracking goods — what you have, what you bought, what you sold, what is left, and what it is worth.

### Stock Groups

Like ledgers have groups, stock items have Stock Groups. Stock Groups classify your inventory — for example, "Electronics," "Stationery," "Raw Materials." Create them at: Inventory Info → Stock Groups → Create.

### Units of Measure

Before creating stock items, create units of measurement. Go to Inventory Info → Units of Measure → Create.
Examples: Nos (Numbers/Pieces), Kg (Kilograms), Ltr (Litres), Box, Pcs, Mtr (Metres).

You can create compound units: 1 Box = 12 Nos. Tally handles conversions automatically.

### Stock Items

Each product you buy or sell is a Stock Item. Go to Inventory Info → Stock Items → Create.

**Name:** Product name. Example: "A4 Printing Paper 500 Sheets."
**Under (Stock Group):** Select the appropriate group.
**Units:** Select the unit (Ream, Box, Pcs).
**GST Applicable:** Yes. Select the appropriate HSN code and GST rate.
**Opening Balance (Stock):** If you have existing inventory, enter quantity and value.

### Godowns (Warehouses)

If your business has multiple storage locations — a main warehouse, a showroom, a branch — create Godowns in Tally to track inventory location.

### Inventory Reports

**Stock Summary:** Current stock position — every item, quantity on hand, value.
**Movement Analysis:** How fast items are moving — turnover rate.
**Reorder Level Report:** Items that have fallen below the minimum stock level you set.
**Ageing Analysis:** How long stock has been sitting — identifies slow-moving or dead stock.
**Batch Report:** If using batch tracking, shows batch-wise stock with expiry dates.

### Valuation Methods

Tally supports multiple inventory valuation methods. The method you choose affects the cost of goods sold and therefore profit.

**FIFO (First In, First Out):** Oldest stock is assumed to be sold first. Most common for perishable goods.
**LIFO (Last In, First Out):** Most recently purchased stock is sold first. Less common, not allowed under Indian GAAP.
**Average Cost:** Stock is valued at the weighted average purchase price. Most commonly used in India.
**Standard Cost:** Stock is valued at a predetermined standard cost, with variances tracked separately.

## 12. GST in Tally

GST is the most important compliance feature in Tally for Indian businesses. Understanding GST thoroughly is essential for anyone working with Tally professionally.

### What Is GST?

GST (Goods and Services Tax) is India's comprehensive indirect tax that replaced a complex system of multiple taxes (VAT, Service Tax, Excise, CST) with a single unified tax. It was implemented on July 1, 2017.

GST has multiple rates: 0% (essential goods), 5%, 12%, 18%, and 28% (luxury/demerit goods). Every good or service is classified under an HSN (Harmonized System of Nomenclature) code for goods or SAC (Services Accounting Code) for services, which determines the applicable GST rate.

**CGST (Central GST):** Collected by the Central Government on intra-state transactions. Half the total GST rate.
**SGST (State GST):** Collected by the State Government on intra-state transactions. Half the total GST rate.
**IGST (Integrated GST):** Collected on inter-state transactions. Full GST rate. Distributed between Centre and State.

Example: Selling goods within Maharashtra at 18% GST → 9% CGST + 9% SGST.
Selling goods from Maharashtra to Delhi at 18% GST → 18% IGST.

### Setting Up GST in Tally

**Step 1: Enable GST in Company Features (F11).**
Enter your GSTIN, state, applicable date.

**Step 2: Create GST Tax Ledgers.**
You need separate ledgers for CGST, SGST, and IGST — both for input (purchases) and output (sales).

Create ledger "CGST Output" under group "Duties & Taxes," Tax Type: GST, select CGST.
Create ledger "SGST Output" under group "Duties & Taxes," Tax Type: GST, select SGST.
Create ledger "IGST Output" under group "Duties & Taxes," Tax Type: GST, select IGST.
Create corresponding Input versions for purchases.

**Step 3: Configure Stock Items with HSN and GST Rate.**
In each stock item, enable GST details, enter the HSN code, select the GST rate. Tally will then automatically calculate the correct GST whenever the item is bought or sold.

**Step 4: Configure Customer/Supplier Ledgers.**
Mark each party as Registered/Unregistered. For registered parties, enter their GSTIN. Tally uses this to determine whether CGST+SGST or IGST applies.

### GST Reports in Tally

**GSTR-1:** Report of all outward supplies (your sales). Filed monthly or quarterly. Tally generates this report exactly as required for filing on the GST portal.

**GSTR-3B:** Summary return filed monthly showing GST liability and input tax credit. Tally generates the figures.

**GSTR-2A/2B Reconciliation:** Tally can reconcile your purchase records against what your suppliers have declared, identifying mismatches that could affect your input tax credit.

**GST Audit Report:** For businesses requiring GST audit.

### Input Tax Credit (ITC)

When you purchase goods or services and pay GST, that GST paid (Input Tax) can be offset against the GST you collect on sales (Output Tax). The net amount is paid to the government.

Output GST collected – Input GST paid = GST payable to government.

Tally tracks this automatically. The "Electronic Cash Ledger" and "Electronic Credit Ledger" concepts in GST are mirrored in Tally's GST reports.

### Common GST Entries

**Local Purchase (Intra-State):**
Dr Purchases ₹10,000
Dr CGST Input ₹900 (9%)
Dr SGST Input ₹900 (9%)
Cr Supplier ₹11,800

**Local Sale (Intra-State):**
Dr Customer ₹17,700
Cr Sales ₹15,000
Cr CGST Output ₹1,350 (9%)
Cr SGST Output ₹1,350 (9%)

**Interstate Sale:**
Dr Customer ₹17,700
Cr Sales ₹15,000
Cr IGST Output ₹2,700 (18%)

## 13. TDS and TCS in Tally

### TDS — Tax Deducted at Source

TDS is a mechanism where the payer deducts tax at the source of payment itself, before paying the recipient. If you pay rent of ₹50,000 to a landlord, you deduct TDS at 10%, pay the landlord ₹45,000, and deposit ₹5,000 with the government.

TDS applies to salary, rent, professional fees, contract payments, interest, and many other payment types. Different rates apply to different payment types.

**Setting Up TDS in Tally:**
Enable TDS in Company Features (F11) → Statutory Features.
Create TDS Nature of Payments (the categories — 194A Interest, 194C Contractor, 194I Rent, etc.) with the applicable rates.
Create a TDS ledger under "Duties & Taxes."
In the relevant expense ledger (e.g., Rent), configure TDS settings.

**Recording a TDS Payment Entry:**
When you record a payment of rent ₹50,000 with 10% TDS:
Dr Rent ₹50,000
Cr Bank ₹45,000
Cr TDS Payable ₹5,000

When you pay TDS to the government:
Dr TDS Payable ₹5,000
Cr Bank ₹5,000

**TDS Reports:** Tally generates Form 26Q, Form 27Q (for foreign payments), and the data needed for filing TDS returns.

### TCS — Tax Collected at Source

TCS is the reverse of TDS — the seller collects tax from the buyer at the point of sale for specific goods (scrap, timber, minerals, motor vehicles above ₹10 lakhs, etc.).

Setup and recording is similar to TDS but from the seller's perspective.

## 14. Payroll in Tally

Tally's payroll module handles salary calculation, attendance tracking, leave management, and statutory deductions for employees.

### Setting Up Payroll

Enable Payroll in F11 → Accounting Features → Maintain Payroll.

**Create Employee Groups:** Organize employees by department — Administration, Sales, Production, etc.

**Create Employees:** For each employee, enter name, date of joining, designation, bank account details, PAN number, PF account number.

**Define Pay Heads:** Pay heads are salary components. Tally has pre-defined pay heads: Basic Salary, House Rent Allowance (HRA), Dearness Allowance (DA), Conveyance Allowance, Medical Allowance, PF (deduction), ESI (deduction), Professional Tax (deduction), Income Tax (deduction).

You can create custom pay heads for any additional components.

**Define Salary Structures:** A salary structure maps pay heads to calculation methods for a group of employees. For example: Basic = ₹25,000 fixed, HRA = 40% of Basic, PF = 12% of Basic.

### Processing Payroll

At month end:
Gateway → Payroll Vouchers → Payroll → Process Payroll.
Select the period, employee group. Tally calculates each employee's gross salary, deductions, and net pay based on attendance.

Tally generates the payroll voucher automatically, crediting each employee's salary payable account and debiting the appropriate expense accounts.

### Statutory Compliance

**PF (Provident Fund):** Tally calculates both employee and employer contributions (12% each on basic + DA). Generates ECR (Electronic Challan cum Return) for EPFO filing.

**ESI (Employee State Insurance):** Applicable for employees earning up to ₹21,000/month. Employee contribution: 0.75%, Employer: 3.25%.

**Professional Tax:** State-specific slab-based tax deducted from salary.

**Form 16:** Annual TDS certificate for employees. Tally generates this.

Bank Reconciliation Statement (BRS) matches your Tally bank ledger balance with the actual bank statement balance. Differences arise because cheques you have issued haven't been cleared yet, or deposits haven't been credited.

In Tally: Gateway → Banking → Bank Reconciliation → Select Bank Ledger.

### Post-Dated Cheques (PDC)

Record cheques with future dates as Post-Dated Cheques. Tally tracks them separately and processes them as effective entries on the cheque date.

## 16. Reports in Tally

Tally's most powerful feature is its ability to generate comprehensive financial reports instantly from the data you have entered. No manual preparation is needed — every report updates in real time as you record transactions.

### Primary Financial Statements

**Trial Balance**
Lists all ledger balances — debit balances and credit balances. Total debits must equal total credits. If they don't, there is an error in data entry. This is the first report to check when troubleshooting.
Path: Gateway → Display More Reports → Trial Balance (or Ctrl+G → "Trial Balance" in TallyPrime)

**Profit and Loss Account**
Shows all income and expenses for a period, resulting in Net Profit or Net Loss. In Tally's P&L format: Trading Account (sales minus cost of goods sold = Gross Profit), then Profit & Loss Account (Gross Profit minus Indirect Expenses + Other Income = Net Profit).
Path: Gateway → Profit & Loss A/c

**Balance Sheet**
Snapshot of assets, liabilities, and capital at a specific date. Must balance (Assets = Liabilities + Capital). Tally's Balance Sheet drills down — click any figure to see the ledgers and individual transactions behind it. This drill-down capability is one of Tally's most useful features.
Path: Gateway → Balance Sheet

**Cash Flow Statement**
Shows cash inflows and outflows classified as Operating, Investing, and Financing activities. Tally generates this automatically from your transaction data.

### Subsidiary Reports

**Day Book:** Every transaction recorded for a day or date range, in chronological order. The most commonly checked report for day-to-day verification.
Path: Gateway → Display More Reports → Day Book

**Ledger:** Complete transaction history of any single ledger for a period. Click any entry to see the original voucher.
Path: Gateway → Display More Reports → Account Books → Ledger

**Cash Book / Bank Book:** All cash or bank transactions for a period.

**Sales Register:** All sales invoices for a period — total sales, GST collected, customer-wise.

**Purchase Register:** All purchase invoices for a period.

### Outstanding Reports

**Bills Receivable:** All outstanding invoices that customers owe you, aged by how long they've been outstanding.
**Bills Payable:** All outstanding invoices you owe to suppliers.
**Creditors Ageing:** Suppliers grouped by how long you've owed them — 0-30 days, 31-60 days, 61-90 days, 90+ days.
**Debtors Ageing:** Customers grouped similarly.

These are essential for cash flow management — knowing who owes you money and how overdue it is.

### Inventory Reports

**Stock Summary:** Current stock on hand — every item, quantity, value.
**Movement Analysis:** Item-wise purchase and sales analysis over a period.
**Stock Transfer:** Movements between godowns.
**Negative Stock Report:** Items showing negative stock (indicating a data entry error — you cannot sell what you do not have in accounting reality).

### GST Reports

**GSTR-1:** Outward supplies (sales) summary — ready to file.
**GSTR-3B:** Monthly summary return figures.
**GST Reconciliation:** Matching your records with the government portal.
**HSN Summary:** Sales and purchases summarized by HSN code.

### Using Drill-Down Navigation

In every Tally report, you can press Enter on any figure to drill down to the next level of detail. From Balance Sheet → click "Sundry Debtors" total → see all debtor ledgers with balances → click a specific debtor → see all outstanding bills → click a bill → see the original voucher. This drill-down from summary to individual transaction is one of the most powerful features of Tally and is used constantly in practice.

## 17. Data Security, Backup, and Restore

### Tally Data Location

Tally stores all company data in a folder on your computer. Default location: C:\Users\[Username]\TallyPrime\Data\ or C:\Tally ERP 9\Data\ (varies by installation). Each company has a numbered subfolder (e.g., 10001, 10002). Inside is a set of files that constitute the company's entire accounting database.

### Taking a Backup

**Manual Backup:**
In Tally: F3 (or Company menu) → Backup → Select Company → Select Destination (USB drive, external hard disk, network folder) → Accept.

Tally compresses and copies the company data files to the destination. The backup file has a .tbk extension.

**Best Practice:** Back up after every session of data entry. Store backups in at least two locations — local and cloud (Google Drive, OneDrive). For businesses, daily automated backups to an offsite location are essential. A corrupted or lost Tally database without backup means losing all accounting data.

### Restoring from Backup

If data is lost or corrupted:
F3 → Restore → Select Source (where backup file is) → Select Company to Restore → Accept.

Tally restores the company data from the backup.

### Password Protection and Security Levels

In TallyPrime: Company Features → Use Security Control → Yes.
Create an Administrator password. Then create additional users with specific permissions — some users can only view reports, some can only enter vouchers, some can alter existing entries. This is essential in businesses with multiple Tally users.

### Tally Vault

Tally Vault is an additional encryption layer. All company data is encrypted with a password. Even if someone copies the data files, they cannot open them without the Vault password. This is separate from the regular security password.

## 21. The Roadmap — How to Learn Tally Step by Step

This is the structured, sequential learning plan. Do not skip phases. Each phase builds on the previous.

### Phase 0: Before Touching the Software (Week 1)

Spend one full week on accounting fundamentals from Section 4 of this guide. You need to genuinely understand the accounting equation, the golden rules of debit and credit, and what a ledger, trial balance, P&L, and balance sheet are before entering a single entry in Tally.

Do not rush this. Every person who struggles with Tally is struggling because of gaps in accounting knowledge, not because Tally is difficult. The software is easy. Accounting is the skill.

Recommended: Solve 20–30 simple accounting problems on paper first. Given a business scenario, identify the two ledgers affected, determine which is debited and which is credited, and record the journal entry manually. This muscle memory will make Tally intuitive.

### Phase 1: Installation and Navigation (Week 2)

Install TallyPrime in Educational Mode. Open it. Spend two hours just exploring without fear of breaking anything — in Educational Mode nothing is permanent unless you save it.

Create your first company: "ABC Traders" with Accounts and Inventory, financial year April 1 to March 31. Explore every menu. Understand where Groups, Ledgers, Stock Groups, Stock Items, and Vouchers are accessed. Learn the keyboard shortcuts — F1 through F10 for voucher types, Ctrl+A to accept (save), Escape to cancel, Alt+C to create masters on the fly.

**Key Milestone for Phase 1:** Be able to navigate to any report, any voucher type, and the masters creation screens confidently and without looking at a reference.

### Phase 2: Masters Creation (Week 3)

Create a complete chart of accounts for a fictional trading business:

Create 5–10 customer ledgers under Sundry Debtors with opening balances.
Create 5–10 supplier ledgers under Sundry Creditors with opening balances.
Create 2 bank ledgers (HDFC Current Account, SBI Savings Account) with opening balances.
Create a Cash ledger (already exists by default).
Create expense ledgers: Rent, Salary, Office Expenses, Electricity, Telephone, Bank Charges, Freight, Advertising.
Create income ledgers: Sales, Other Income.
Create a Capital Account ledger for the owner.
Create 10–15 stock items under appropriate stock groups with units of measure and opening stock.

**Key Milestone for Phase 2:** Your Trial Balance shows correct opening balances. All customer balances are debit. All supplier balances are credit. Bank balances are debit. Capital is credit.

### Phase 3: Recording Transactions (Weeks 4–5)

Now record a full month of transactions for your fictional company. Record at least:

15–20 sales transactions using Sales Vouchers (some cash sales, some credit sales to customer ledgers).
10–15 purchase transactions using Purchase Vouchers (from supplier ledgers).
5–8 payment vouchers (paying rent, salaries, utilities, supplier bills).
5–8 receipt vouchers (receiving payments from customers).
2–3 contra entries (cash to bank, bank to cash).
2–3 journal entries (depreciation, write-off).
2–3 credit notes (sales returns).
2–3 debit notes (purchase returns).

After each day of entries, check the Day Book, the Trial Balance, and individual ledger balances. Verify they make sense.

**Key Milestone for Phase 3:** You can record any type of transaction, the Trial Balance balances, and the P&L shows a realistic gross profit and net profit figure.

### Phase 4: GST Integration (Weeks 6–7)

Redo the company setup (or create a new company) with full GST configuration. Create GST-enabled ledgers, configure stock items with HSN codes and GST rates, and re-record the transactions from Phase 3 with proper GST.

Verify that the GST reports (GSTR-1 and GSTR-3B) are populating correctly. Understand the difference between input tax credit and output tax liability. Understand what "Net GST Payable" means.

**Key Milestone for Phase 4:** The GSTR-1 report matches your sales vouchers exactly. The GSTR-3B shows the correct net GST payable.

### Phase 5: Advanced Features (Weeks 8–10)

Payroll: Create 5 employees with different salary structures. Process one month of payroll. Verify the salary expense in P&L.

Bank Reconciliation: Perform a bank reconciliation for your HDFC Current Account by comparing your Tally bank ledger to a fictional bank statement (create one on paper).

Cost Centres: Enable cost centres. Create two departments — Administration and Sales. Tag all expenses to the appropriate department. Generate a department-wise P&L.

Budgets: Create a budget for the current year. Enter budgeted figures for income and expenses. View the Variance Analysis report comparing actual to budget.

TDS: Enable TDS. Record rent payments with TDS deducted. Verify the TDS payable ledger balance.

**Key Milestone for Phase 5:** You can confidently use all major features of Tally. The financial statements are complete, accurate, and meaningful.

### Phase 6: Data Management and Reporting (Week 11)

Export reports to Excel and PDF. Take a backup of your practice company. Restore it to verify the backup works. Create a second user with limited permissions (view only) and test that the restrictions work. Explore every report in the Display menu — spend time understanding what each report shows and when a business would use it.

**Key Milestone for Phase 6:** You can independently close out a month, generate all compliance reports, back up data, and explain every report to someone else.

## 22. Common Mistakes to Avoid While Learning

These are the mistakes that create bad habits, corrupt data, and cause failures in professional settings.

**Mistake 1: Skipping the Accounting Fundamentals.** The single most common and most damaging mistake. People who skip Section 4 and go straight to the software find themselves recording entries they do not understand, making errors they cannot identify, and building a fundamentally broken set of books. There are no shortcuts — understand accounting first.

**Mistake 2: Selecting the Wrong Group for a Ledger.** If you create a customer under "Sundry Creditors" instead of "Sundry Debtors," the Balance Sheet will be wrong. If you create an expense under "Fixed Assets," the P&L will be wrong. The group is not just a label — it determines the financial statement position of every ledger. Always double-check the group when creating a ledger.

**Mistake 3: Using the Wrong Voucher Type.** Using a Journal voucher instead of a Payment voucher for cash payments, or using a Payment voucher for sales entries. Each voucher type has a specific purpose. Using the wrong one misclassifies the transaction and affects reports.

**Mistake 4: Not Enabling Bill-Wise Details for Debtors and Creditors.** Without bill-wise details, you cannot track which specific invoice is outstanding. You only see a total balance. Enabling this from the beginning (in Company Features) is essential for proper receivables and payables management. It is very difficult to enable this retroactively once transactions are recorded.

**Mistake 5: Not Backing Up Regularly.** Tally data files can become corrupted. Computers can crash. Without a backup, all your work is lost. Develop the habit of backing up after every session of data entry — not just occasionally.

**Mistake 6: Altering Vouchers Carelessly.** In Tally, any voucher can be altered (edited). In a real business, altering a posted voucher — especially one that has been shared with a customer (invoice) or submitted to the government (GST return) — is a serious issue. Develop the discipline to check entries before saving. If a correction is needed, understand whether to alter or to pass a correcting entry.

**Mistake 7: Ignoring the Trial Balance.** The Trial Balance is your first line of defense against errors. Check it after every significant data entry session. If it balances but the figures look wrong, investigate individual ledgers. If it doesn't balance, there is a data integrity error — find and fix it immediately.

**Mistake 8: Not Understanding the Difference Between Cash and Accrual Accounting.** Tally by default uses accrual accounting — revenue is recorded when earned (invoice date), not when cash is received. A common beginner error is only recording transactions when cash moves, missing credit transactions entirely. Record all invoices as they happen, and record payments separately.

**Mistake 9: Treating Tally as a Calculator, Not an Accounting System.** Some people use Tally to record transactions but never look at the reports. The reports are where the value is. After recording transactions, always review the Day Book, check the Trial Balance, and periodically review the P&L and Balance Sheet. This is what makes accounting meaningful.

**Mistake 10: Not Using Narrations.** The narration field in every voucher is for recording a brief description of why the transaction happened. "Rent paid April 2024 cheque no. 001234" is far more useful than a blank narration. Narrations make auditing, reconciliation, and finding specific entries dramatically easier. Fill them in every time.

**Mistake 11: Date Errors.** Entering transactions with wrong dates is surprisingly common. Always press F2 to verify and change the date before entering a voucher. A December transaction entered in January distorts monthly reports, GST returns, and comparisons.

**Mistake 12: Creating Duplicate Ledgers.** Creating "HDFC Bank" and "HDFC Current Account" as two separate ledgers when they are the same account. This splits transactions across two accounts, making the bank balance wrong. Be disciplined about ledger naming conventions and always search before creating.

**Freelance Tally Operator.** Many small businesses prefer to hire a part-time accountant who visits 2–3 times a week to maintain their Tally accounts, rather than a full-time employee. This is a flexible, well-paid freelance opportunity.

**ERP Implementation Consultant.** Helping businesses set up and configure Tally — chart of accounts design, opening balance migration, staff training, GST setup. Consulting rates: ₹500–₹2,000/hour depending on experience.

**Finance Manager / CFO (SME).** In India's SME sector, CFOs and Finance Managers are often directly involved with Tally for management reporting. Tally proficiency at the reporting and analysis level (not just data entry) is valued.

## 26. Glossary of Important Terms

**Accounts Payable:** Money owed by the business to suppliers for goods/services received on credit. Tracked through Sundry Creditors in Tally.

**Accounts Receivable:** Money owed to the business by customers for goods/services delivered on credit. Tracked through Sundry Debtors in Tally.

**Accrual Accounting:** Recording revenue when earned and expenses when incurred, regardless of when cash changes hands.

**Balance Sheet:** Financial statement showing assets, liabilities, and equity at a specific date.

**Bill-Wise Details:** Tally feature that tracks each invoice individually for debtors and creditors, enabling aging analysis.

**BRS (Bank Reconciliation Statement):** Statement explaining differences between the bank ledger balance in Tally and the actual bank statement balance.

**CGST:** Central Goods and Services Tax — component of GST collected by the Central Government on intra-state transactions.

**Chart of Accounts:** The complete list of all ledgers organized by groups.

**Closing Stock:** Value of unsold inventory at the end of a period.

**Cost Centre:** A department, project, or division used to track income and expenses separately for management analysis.

**Credit Note:** Document issued to a customer when they return goods — reduces the customer's outstanding balance.

**Debit Note:** Document sent to a supplier when goods are returned — reduces the amount payable to them.

**Depreciation:** Systematic allocation of an asset's cost over its useful life. Recorded as a Journal entry in Tally.

**Double Entry:** Accounting system where every transaction affects at least two accounts equally.

**E-Invoice:** Electronically authenticated invoice generated via the government's Invoice Registration Portal. Mandatory for businesses above a certain turnover.

**E-Way Bill:** Electronic document required for movement of goods valued above ₹50,000. Generated through the government portal or directly from TallyPrime.

**ERP:** Enterprise Resource Planning — integrated software managing all business functions.

**FIFO:** First In, First Out inventory valuation method.

**Financial Year:** April 1 to March 31 in India.

**GSTR-1:** Monthly/Quarterly return of outward supplies (sales). Filed on the GST portal.

**GSTR-3B:** Monthly summary GST return showing liability and input tax credit.

**GSTIN:** 15-character GST Identification Number assigned to each registered business.

**Godown:** Storage location for inventory in Tally. A business can have multiple godowns.

**Group:** Category in Tally's chart of accounts hierarchy that contains ledgers.

**HSN Code:** Harmonized System of Nomenclature — 6 to 8 digit code classifying goods for GST purposes.

**IGST:** Integrated Goods and Services Tax — collected on inter-state transactions.

**ITC (Input Tax Credit):** GST paid on purchases that can be offset against GST payable on sales.

**Journal Entry:** Record of a financial transaction showing debits and credits.

**Ledger:** Individual account in Tally for any entity (customer, supplier, bank, expense type, income type).

**MRP:** Maximum Retail Price — can be configured in Tally for stock items.

**Opening Balance:** Account balance at the start of a financial year or at company creation.

**P&L (Profit and Loss Account):** Financial statement showing all income and expenses resulting in net profit or loss for a period.

**PAN:** Permanent Account Number — unique tax identification number issued by Income Tax department.

**Party Ledger:** Ledger for a customer or supplier (as opposed to an account ledger for cash, bank, or expenses).

**Payroll:** Process of calculating and recording employee salaries and statutory deductions.

**SAC Code:** Services Accounting Code — used to classify services for GST.

**SGST:** State Goods and Services Tax — component collected by the State Government on intra-state transactions.

**Stock Item:** An individual product in Tally's inventory system.

**Sundry Creditors:** Suppliers to whom the business owes money. A liability on the Balance Sheet.

**Sundry Debtors:** Customers who owe money to the business. An asset on the Balance Sheet.

**TCS:** Tax Collected at Source — tax collected by the seller from the buyer on specific goods.

**TDS:** Tax Deducted at Source — tax deducted by the payer from the payee for specific payment types.

**Trial Balance:** List of all ledger balances — total debits must equal total credits.

**Tally Vault:** Encryption feature in Tally that protects company data files with a password.

**Voucher:** Record of a financial transaction in Tally. Equivalent to a journal entry.

## Final Note: The Mindset for Mastering Tally

Tally is not hard. Accounting is the skill — Tally is the tool. Once you understand what a debit is and what a credit is, once you understand why the balance sheet must balance, once you understand that every transaction has two sides — Tally becomes almost self-explanatory.

The accountants who master Tally are not those who memorize keyboard shortcuts. They are those who deeply understand what they are recording, why it goes where it goes, and what the reports tell them about the financial health of a business.

Tally is the financial backbone of millions of Indian businesses. Every transaction in a shop, every invoice in a factory, every salary in a company, every tax return filed — all of it flows through Tally. Learning it is not just learning software. It is learning the language of business finance in India.

Start with the fundamentals. Build the theory. Then open Tally and build something real.
